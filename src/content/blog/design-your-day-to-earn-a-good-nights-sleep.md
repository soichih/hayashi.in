I used to think of sleep as maintenance — you refuel at night so you can work the next day. Lately I've started to wonder if it's the other way around.

What if the day is the means, and the night is the end? We work, eat, resolve our small conflicts, tie off our loose threads — not to prepare for tomorrow's labor, but to earn a night of undisturbed sleep. The day is in service of the rest, not the other way around.

There's a version of this in the old idea of dying at peace: that a life well spent is one you can set down without regret. Scale that down to a single day, and the question changes shape. Not "did I get everything done," but "can I close my eyes without an unresolved argument, an unspoken apology, an unfinished worry, tugging at me?"

Maybe that's the real measure of a day — not its productivity, but its ability to let you rest. Each day, in this frame, becomes a small rehearsal for the ending we all eventually write: did I leave things a little better than I found them, so I can sleep, and so whoever comes after can sleep a little easier too.

## Further reading

- Michael K. Scullin et al., ["The Effects of Bedtime Writing on Difficulty Falling Asleep"](https://news.web.baylor.edu/news/story/2018/can-writing-your-dos-help-you-doze-baylor-study-suggests-jotting-down-tasks-can) (Baylor University, 2018) — a polysomnographic study finding that writing down a to-do list at bedtime helped people fall asleep faster than writing down tasks already completed, suggesting it's the *unfinished* over the *done* that keeps the mind occupied at night.
- ["Interruption, recall and resumption: a meta-analysis of the Zeigarnik and Ovsiankina effects"](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05000-w) (*Humanities and Social Sciences Communications*, 2025) — the classic finding that unfinished tasks are recalled and intrude on thought more persistently than completed ones.
- Carney & Waters, ["Effects of a structured problem-solving procedure on pre-sleep cognitive arousal in college students with insomnia"](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16390282/) (2006) — evidence for "constructive worry": deliberately working through open problems earlier in the evening reduces the racing mind that shows up once the lights are off.
- Seneca, on Sextius's nightly practice, in [*De Ira* III.36](https://garenhart.com/judgment/seneca-daily-judgment-seat/) — the Stoic habit of asking each night, before sleep, "what bad habit have you cured today, what fault have you resisted, in what respect are you better?"
- ["Reflections on mortality can help you live well now"](https://psyche.co/guides/reflections-on-mortality-can-help-you-live-well-now-heres-how) (*Psyche*, Aeon) — on memento mori as a daily discipline, the same scaling-down of a whole-life question ('did I live well') onto a single day that this post borrows for sleep.
